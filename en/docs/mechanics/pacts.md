# Pacts

## What a pact is

A pact is a single-use, supernaturally binding agreement between a person and an entity drawn to their despair. It functions mechanically like a powerful one-use feat. It functions narratively like a prayer answered by something that was already listening.

Pacts are not a class feature. Anyone can make one — class-bearer or not. The only requirement is sufficient desperation.

---

## How pacts work

Pact granters are drawn to despair. They do not seek out the powerful or the worthy. They find people at the end of their options.

The terms of a pact are always **completely clear** to both parties at the moment of agreement. There are no corrupted wishes. No malicious interpretations. No small print. If you agree to survive the battle at the cost of your companions' lives, you understand exactly what you are agreeing to. The tragedy of pacts does not come from being cheated — it comes from knowing the price and paying it anyway.

### Structure

Every pact has:

- **The offering** — what the petitioner receives. Always unambiguous.
- **The price** — what the petitioner pays. Always unambiguous. May be immediate or ongoing.
- **The binding** — once agreed, the pact cannot be undone. Attempts to cheat it do not produce loopholes. They produce insanity.

### Price types

| Type        | Description                                             |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| One-off     | Paid once, immediately or at a defined future moment    |
| Ongoing     | Paid continuously for the rest of the petitioner's life |
| Conditional | Triggered by a specific future event                    |

Prices are never purely material. The pact system is not a transaction — it is a karmic weight. Prices that seem material (gold, land, an object) always carry a narrative cost that outlasts the payment.

---

## Example pact

> **Offering:** You will survive the battle.
> **Price:** None of your companions will.
> **Binding:** The petitioner survives. Their companions do not. The petitioner will have to live with the knowledge that they chose this.

Note what this is not: the petitioner does not survive as a prisoner, or survive maimed beyond function, or survive in a way that renders survival meaningless. They survive. The price is the price — not a punishment disguised as one.

---

## Pact granters

Pact granters are not named, statted, or fully described in the base rules. They are drawn to despair and they offer what is within their power to offer. What they are, what they want, why they respond — these are setting questions.

What the rules specify:

- They exist
- They listen
- They are honest
- They are not merciful

## Kai intersection

A spellcaster who has overdrawn their mana to zero is in precisely the condition that draws pact granters. A pact that offers one final overdraw — at permanent kai cost as the price — is structurally coherent with both systems.

This means kai degradation from overdraw and pact prices can compound. A character who makes a pact to survive by burning their kai is not just spending a resource — they are becoming less of what they were, permanently, knowingly.

---

## Cheating a pact

Characters who attempt to find loopholes, technically comply while violating intent, or otherwise cheat the terms of a pact are driven to insanity. This is not a GM ruling. It is a property of the binding.

The insanity is not a mechanical status effect. It is a narrative consequence that the GM adjudicates — but its direction is always toward the spirit of the original price. The universe does not forget what was agreed.

!!! warning

    This mechanic exists to enforce the design principle that tragedy comes from known prices, not betrayal. If the GM is using pact loopholes to punish players, the mechanic is being misused.
